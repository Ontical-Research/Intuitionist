Theorem. Irrationality of \(\sqrt{2}\)
\(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.
Proof.
Suppose for contradiction that \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational.
Then \(\sqrt{2} = a/b\) for some \(a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\) with \(b \neq 0\) and \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\).
1 Squaring, \(a^2 = 2b^2\).

\(2 \mid a\)

Intuitionist
I can see you're trying to show 2 ∣ a and omega didn't work. That makes sense — omega handles linear arithmetic, but divisibility is a different kind of claim.
Current proof state
Within contradiction: \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational, so \(\sqrt{2} = a/b\) with \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\).
Established: \(a^2 = 2b^2\)
Need to show: \(2 \mid a\)
What do you know about how primes interact with products and squares?
You
If a prime divides a product then it divides one of the factors? Is that what I need here?
Intuitionist
Exactly — that's Euclid's lemma. And \(a^2 = a \cdot a\) is a product. So if \(2 \mid a^2\), then \(2 \mid a\). In Lean, this is in Mathlib as Int.Prime.dvd_of_dvd_sq. You'll need to tell Lean that 2 is prime (which it can figure out) and that \(2 \mid a^2\) (which follows from hsq). Try something like:
exact Int.Prime.dvd_of_dvd_sq ⟨hsq ▸ dvd_mul_right _ _⟩
You
Wait, I want to make sure I understand. Why does 2 | a² follow from hsq? Can you walk me through that?
Intuitionist
Good question. Look at what hsq says:
Hypothesis hsq
\(a^2 = 2 \cdot b^2\)
The right-hand side is \(2 \cdot (\text{something})\). By definition, that means \(2\) divides the left-hand side. So \(2 \mid a^2\) is literally just "the RHS of hsq has 2 as a factor." In Lean terms, dvd_mul_right 2 (b ^ 2) gives you \(2 \mid 2 \cdot b^2\), and hsq ▸ rewrites \(2 \cdot b^2\) to \(a^2\).

C
Chat Mode — Philosophical Digression
The conversation goes deeper. The student asks about why the proof works. This is where learning happens.
Intuitionist — Irrationality.lean
Irrationality.lean
1 theorem sqrt2_irrational :
2 Irrational (Real.sqrt 2) := by
3 by_contra h
4 rw [not_irrational_iff] at h
5 obtain ⟨a, b, hb, hab⟩ := h
6 have hcop := Int.Coprime.of_reduced a b
7 have hsq : a ^ 2 = 2 * b ^ 2 := by
8 field_simp at hab
9 linarith [sq_nonneg a, sq_nonneg b]
10 have ha : 2 ∣ a := by
11 exact Int.Prime.dvd_of_dvd_sq ⟨hsq ▸ dvd_mul_right _ _⟩
12 obtain ⟨k, hk⟩ := ha
13
Hyps a b k : ℤ hb : b ≠ 0 hcop : gcd a b = 1 hsq : a² = 2b² hk : a = 2k
Goal ⊥
Paper ⌘1
Chat ⌘2
You
Ok that worked. Now I have a = 2k and I need ⊥. This is the same trick again right? I substitute back and show 2 | b?
Intuitionist
Exactly the same structure. Substitute \(a = 2k\) into \(a^2 = 2b^2\), simplify, and you'll get \(2 \mid b\). Then you have \(2 \mid a\) and \(2 \mid b\), which contradicts \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\).
You
Something bugs me about this proof. It feels like a trick. We just happened to notice that 2 divides both. Is there a deeper reason why √2 is irrational, or is it really just "assume rational, do some algebra, find a contradiction"?
Intuitionist
That's a really good instinct. The "trick" you're noticing is actually a specific instance of a general phenomenon: the unique factorization of integers. The deep reason \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational is that in the equation \(a^2 = 2b^2\), the left side has an even number of prime factors (everything is squared), but the right side has an odd number (there's that extra 2). Unique factorization says this is impossible. This proof by infinite descent — showing both \(a\) and \(b\) are even, so the fraction wasn't reduced — is actually the same argument, just wearing different clothes. The descent can't terminate, which is another way of saying the prime factorization doesn't work out.
Proof structure so far
Assumed \(\sqrt{2} = a/b\) in lowest terms
Derived \(2 \mid a\), wrote \(a = 2k\)
Next: substitute back, derive \(2 \mid b\) → contradiction
Incidentally, this is why the same style of proof works for \(\sqrt{3}\), \(\sqrt{5}\), any \(\sqrt{p}\) for prime \(p\). The "trick" generalizes because the underlying reason is structural.
You
Oh that's beautiful. The parity of the exponents. Can you prove √2 irrational that way in Lean too — via unique factorization directly?
Intuitionist
You can! Mathlib has Nat.Multiplicity and the fundamental theorem of arithmetic. The proof via unique factorization is actually shorter if you're willing to use heavier Mathlib machinery. But there's a pedagogical reason to do it the way you're doing it: the descent argument is constructive. You're actually building the witnesses — here's why \(a\) is even, here's why \(b\) is even. The unique factorization proof is more conceptual but less hands-on. For learning Lean and learning proof, the descent is better. Finish this version first. Then if you want, we can try the other approach as a second proof.
Ask about your proof…
This is the learning conversation. The student went from "how do I fix this tactic" to "why does this proof work" to "is there a deeper approach." The agent followed, weaving between Lean specifics and mathematical insight. Notice: the agent quotes the proof structure mid-conversation to anchor the abstract discussion in the concrete proof. The Quote button lets the student do this too — "here's where I am, help me think about this part." The chat and the proof aren't separate activities; they're two views of the same process.
